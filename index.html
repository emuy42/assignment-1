US Humanities Professors Fight for the Discipline

To counter a historic decline in enrollment, faculty and researchers are out to prove that humanities graduates can thrive

By Elisa Muyl

Oct. 12, 2023

In September, an impassioned panel of faculty at colleges and universities in New York testified for hours on the value of a humanities education in response to what they called mounting attacks on the discipline.

“We’re not loud enough,” said Lehman professor Karin Beck, whose piece The Humanities Aren’t Hurting Everywhere prompted the hearing. “We have to speak up so they can’t pretend they didn’t hear us.”

Between 2012 and 2021, undergraduate enrollment in the humanities in the US declined by as much as 30 percent by some measures. Meanwhile, the number of students flocking to career-oriented fields has dramatically increased since 2000: business (60 percent), engineering (100 percent) and health-related majors (200 percent). The American Academy of Arts & Sciences, the organization that published this data, connects the decline in humanities enrollment to periods of economic anxiety, such as the 2008 recession or the outbreak of COVID-19.

The perception that graduates with humanities degrees struggle to find gainful employment has direct implications for the availability of humanities courses on US campuses. In March 2021, a Florida state senator introduced a bill that would tie students’ financial aid to the employability of their majors. In September 2023, West Virginia University eliminated 28 majors, effectively gutting the school’s liberal-arts program, to address a $45m budget shortfall.
